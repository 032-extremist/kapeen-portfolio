Teaching a model to read scans: notes on Medical AI

Medical AI started as a question: could I take publicly available imaging datasets and train models that actually distinguish, say, a pneumonia-positive chest X-ray from a healthy one — and then wrap that in something a non-technical person could use? The answer was yes, but the path there taught me more about evaluation than about modeling.

Three problems, three models

The app ended up serving three separate classifiers: one for brain tumor detection, one for pneumonia, and one for retinal conditions. Each was trained and saved as its own .h5 model file, and the Flask app routes incoming images to the right one based on what the user is checking for. Keeping them as separate models, rather than one combined network, made each one easier to debug and re-train independently.

Accuracy is the wrong first metric

The biggest lesson here had nothing to do with TensorFlow syntax. On medical data, a model that's 95% "accurate" can still be useless if it's wrong in the direction that matters — missing real positives. I had to actually look at the confusion matrix, not just the headline accuracy number, before I trusted any of these models enough to put them behind an interface.

Serving a trained model is its own skill

Training a model in a notebook and serving it reliably from a Flask endpoint are different problems. I had to think about: how the uploaded image gets preprocessed to match exactly what the model was trained on, what happens if someone uploads something that isn't a valid scan, and how to keep the .h5 files loading fast enough that the app doesn't feel sluggish on every request.

What's next

  • Add confidence scores to the output, not just a label — a flat "positive/negative" hides how sure the model actually is.
  • Build a small evaluation report for each model so the numbers are visible, not just trusted.
  • Look into model compression so the app starts faster on free-tier hosting.

This one's a reminder that "data analysis" isn't just charts — it's the discipline of not trusting a number until you've checked what's behind it.
